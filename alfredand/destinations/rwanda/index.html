Rwanda
Tailor-made Rwanda Holidays

When it comes to exuberant, mood-enhancing greenery, Rwanda is in a league of its own. Crammed within the borders of mainland Africa’s fourth-smallest country, there are ancient jungle-smothered volcanoes where mountain gorillas roam, lush montane forests that vibrate with the cries of rare primates and expansive wetlands where the birdlife is so abundant it hardly seems plausible. Even the boundless blue lake on its western border is studded with tiny emerald isles. And there’s another type of ‘greenness’ worthy of note: the ban on plastic bags since 2008 and mandatory once-a-month community clean-ups that have turned this into one of the continent’s cleanest states, and the National Parks’ revenue-sharing schemes that give back to the locals. 

There will be inspiring discoveries, moments and encounters wherever you go in this verdant country, and the hour you spend in the company of a mighty silverback and his family will certainly be one of them. But the longer you stay here, the more it will be Rwanda’s resilient people who impress you with their warmth and dignity – all the more remarkable in the light of their tragic and relatively recent history. Wherever you go and whoever you meet, from the enthusiastic foodie who shows you Kigali’s growing food scene to the artisans keeping the country’s rich craft traditions alive in the villages or the patient workers on a tea plantation, their shared quiet pride in their homeland and in what they have achieved together since the dark days of 1994 will be the thing that most touches your heart.

Three years later, inspired by her foodie upbringing, she founded Heaven, a restaurant and hospitality training facility which has helped more than 1,500 Rwandans to date. Over the years, the Heaven portfolio has expanded to include hotels, restaurants, tours and experiences, a journey partly documented in husband Josh’s book, A Thousand Hills to Heaven.

    Tourism and Hospitality Manager at Akagera National Park

    Rwandan-born Ian Munyankindi has been with African Parks since 2012. Prior to this, he gained his hospitality expertise in some of Rwanda’s best hotels and worked as a freelance guide. He joined the Akagera team as manager of Ruzizi Tented Lodge and has worked his way up to Tourism and Hospitality Manager. Ian’s passion for wildlife ensures that guests depart with a deep appreciation of the beauty of Akagera National Park – which he jokingly refers to as his wife!

  • Protais Niyigaba

    Nyungwe Park Manager and CEO

    Born in one of the districts bordering Nyungwe, Protais Niyigaba grew up with a passion for conservation. Since completing his MSc in Biodiversity Conservation from the University of Rwanda, he has undertaken various roles in his field. He joined Nyungwe Management Company in June 2021 as Park Manager, having previously been a guide and nature interpreter there. A keen birder and botanist, he enjoys working and living in his favourite environment and being paid for doing what he loves. 

  • Micheline Habineza

    As Question Coffee's Director of Operations, Micheline Habineza oversees, manages and grows multiple areas of the social enterprise, including the Academy, Café, Wholesale, Roasting and Quality Control. She joined the company as a Wholesale Account Coordinator in 2016, before taking on the position of Retail Operations Manager. Prior to that, she spent three-and-a-half years with the international in-flight catering company ASL Rwanda EPZE in Kigali as their customer liaison.

  • Marie Aimee Umugeni

    Managing director of Umutima Cooperative at Nyamirambo Women’s Center

    Marie Aimee Umugeni is one of the co-founders of the Nyamirambo Women’s Centre in Kigali, which was set up in 2007 to provide support to local women. It offers literacy and computer classes and teaches tailoring, enabling participants to work at the centre producing items for its Umutima Cooperative, of which Marie Aimee is managing director.

Time difference: GMT +2

Flight time: London + 11-12 hours

Currency: Rwandan Franc (RWF)

Language: Kinyarwandan, English, French and Swahili

Electricity: 230v

Telephone code: + 250

Tipping: Tipping is not compulsory in Rwanda although it is customary to add 10% to a restaurant bill if you have had good service. Key to your tour of Rwanda is likely to be your driver-guide therefore as a general guideline, allow for USD$10-15 per day.

When gorilla trekking, you will normally be accompanied by a team of people. The national park ranger and primate guide should be tipped separately (USD$10 each). If you chose to hire a porter to carry your day pack and camera equipment, a tip of USD$3 per person is usual. An advance party of trackers will be ahead of you to find the gorillas. A tip of USD$5-10USD for the tracking team is reasonable.

Etiquette: The horrific genocide of 1994 is still raw. People identify as Rwandans therefore it is impolite to ask people of their ethnicity and whether they are Hutu or Tutsi. Many of the people you will meet will have first-hand experience of the genocide but do not ask them for their personal stories. If they want to tell you, they will. A period of mourning is observed annually in April.

Photography is prohibited of government buildings and permission should always be asked when taking photos of people. Your trekking guide will explain the behaviour expected when observing gorillas.

Religions: Christianity is by far the most popular denomination in Rwanda with 90% of worshippers. Over half of the population follow Roman Catholicism which was introduced to the country in the early 1900s by missionaries. Many Rwandans also practice indigenous beliefs with rituals and offerings made to their deceased ancestors.

Use of plastic: The law bans the use, manufacture, and importation of all single-use plastic bags. Travellers arriving in Rwanda with duty-free plastic bags will be required to leave them at the airport. The government however confirms that reusable zip lock bags are permitted. Plastic bottled water may be provided in vehicles although to stay hydrated on the go, we recommend travelling with a personal, reusable bottle which you can refill at airports, hotels and lodges.

Visas: You can look up the latest advice on https://www.gov.uk/foreign-travel-advice/rwanda/entry-requirements. Please be aware that this information can change at short notice.

Vaccinations: Health risks vary depending on the destination so it’s essential to check the specific vaccination and hygiene requirements for Rwanda on the NHS Fit For Travel website.

Lying to the south of the Equator, Rwanda has a tropical climate and has two rainy seasons each year. The first is usually from mid-February to May and the second is from mid-September to mid-December.

Virunga National Park, due to its altitude, is the coolest part of the country and whilst the dry season from June to mid-September is the most popular time for gorilla trekking, the availability of gorilla trekking permits can determine when you travel as they are limited in numbers and demand often exceeds supply. Gorilla trekking is year-round, but due to the mountainous conditions you can expect to get wet and muddy at any time.

Akagera National Park is best visited during the dry season from June to September when the animals are more visible around the waterholes.

The ‘Land of a Thousand Hills’ is easiest explored by road. Most guests opt for a driver-guide who will travel with them throughout. It’s a great way of seeing the beauty of the country with its steep sided mountains and verdant landscaped terraces.  All major towns and cities are connected by a good road network. Due to its volcanic landscape, the roads can be very twisty so remember to take tablets if you suffer from motion sickness.

In Kigali, taxi services are available. However motorbike taxis, used by the locals, are not likely to be covered on your travel insurance. The country does not have a rail network.

In Akagera National Park, safaris are taken in 4×4 vehicles whilst boat cruises are a popular excursion to take on Lake Kivu.  For an aerial perspective of Rwanda’s varied topography, helicopter tours and transfers can be arranged.

Kigali, Rwanda’s cosmopolitan capital, offers a multitude of international menus including Italian, Japanese, Korean and Indian. Plantain, often in its steamed and mashed form of matoke, is a regular feature on Rwandan menus.  Meat tends to be served brochette style; skewered cubes of beef, chicken or goat accompanied with fries or rice.  Tilapia is a common fish dish particularly if you are visiting Lake Kivu where you can see the fishermen go out daily and return with their catch.

Local bars serve urwagwa, a traditionally brewed beer. It’s an acquired taste as is banana wine. Some of the finest tea and coffee is produced in Rwanda and Kigali has a plethora of coffee-shops where you can stop and watch the world go by as you sip on a frothy cappuccino.

To get a flavour of what you can buy, Caplaki Craft Village in Kigali is a great place to start your Rwandan shopping experience. Here, individual stalls sell a range of souvenirs and gifts including wood carvings, clothing, pottery, masks, jewellery and weavings.

Amahoro (peace) baskets are a popular purchase. These colourful creations have a wide base and a narrow-pointed lid. For something more unusual, imigongo is a traditional Rwandan artwork of bold geometric patterns made from cow dung. Kitenge is the bright fabric worn by the women of Rwanda and the cloth can be bought from many of the local markets or from roadside stalls.  Hand-made beaded jewellery also makes a great gift.

Coffee and tea plantations offer tours and most offer the opportunity to purchase after your tasting session. As you tour the country you may come across small cooperatives set up in conjunction with NGOs. Buying from these stall holders will benefit the local community.

Rwanda’s most famous festival is Kwita Izina. This is an official naming ceremony for all gorillas born in the previous year. Held in Kinigi in Volcanoes National Park on World Gorilla Day on 24th September it is also a tribute to Dian Fossey who started her gorilla studies by creating the Karisoke Research Centre in the Virunga Mountains.

In July Rwanda’s film festival, or ‘Hillywood’ as it is more commonly known, is held in Kigali but also goes on tour throughout the country with pop-up cinemas.

Rwanda is a very clean country, largely due to the compulsory activity of  ‘umuganda’. Translated as “coming together for a common purpose” it’s held on the last Saturday of every month where locals gather to clean their local community.

7th April is Genocide Memorial Day and the start of a week of mourning and a time for reflection for the thousands of victims and their families.  Other public holidays include Independence Day celebrated on 1st July and on the first Friday of August, Umuganaru Day gives thanks for the start of harvest.
